This crossing was fixed in five days.

In April 2026, a pedestrian in Cork measured an unsafe junction with a smartphone. Cork City Council confirmed repairs within five days — and admitted the signal timing parameters hadn't been revised in 13 years. WalkPhase is how it happened.

The Proof

What happened at Killumney Road, Ballincollig.

On Friday, 17 April 2026, a WalkPhase user measured the junction of Killumney Road and Carriganarra Road in Ballincollig, Cork. The platform identified specific safety issues at the crossing and generated a structured safety report. The report was sent to Cork City Council's Traffic Requests team.

Five days later, on Wednesday, 22 April 2026, the Council responded.

Cork City Council — written response · Ref: CSR/2026/08609/RDS

"I got confirmation that two push buttons were repaired at that junction… Another visit is planned to confirm crossing distances and associated times as the parameters for that junction likely haven't been revised since its original installation 13 odd years ago."

— ITS Section, Cork City Council, 22 April 2026

The council's own response acknowledges what WalkPhase was built to expose: signal timing parameters at signalised crossings are routinely set decades ago and rarely revised. The pedestrians who walked the slowest in 2013 are not the same ones using the crossing in 2026.

"If a crossing isn't safe, you shouldn't have to argue. You should be able to prove it."

Emmett Murphy, founder, WalkPhase

The Mechanism

A simple walk, turned into structured safety evidence.

Most pedestrian signals in Ireland — and across the world — are timed on the assumption that pedestrians walk at 1.0 metres per second. Many people don't. Older adults, people with mobility challenges, parents with young children, and many others walk at 0.6 to 0.8 metres per second.

That means the walk phase at some signalised crossings ends while vulnerable people are still in the road.

WalkPhase measures this gap. AI analyses the walk and generates a structured safety report ready to send to the relevant council or road authority.

1

Walk

Approach a signalised crossing. WalkPhase detects the kerb, the wait, and the crossing.

Real walking speed, wait time, signal phase duration. No roadside hardware. No infrastructure required.

AI generates a structured safety report. Send it to the road authority. They have evidence — not a complaint.

The Action

No permission required. Anyone can do this.

WalkPhase doesn't require a city contract, a research permit, or a council approval. Any signalised crossing in Ireland can be measured by anyone with a smartphone.

Walk. Measure. Report.

The Killumney Road crossing was measured by one person. Five days later, two push buttons had been repaired.

The Impact

If it worked at one crossing, it works at every crossing.

Ireland is where WalkPhase first proved it works. Every signalised crossing in the country can now be measured the same way — by residents, by advocates, by councils, by anyone willing to walk.

The longer-term roadmap includes signal-controller integration to automatically extend green-walk time for verified vulnerable road users — older adults, people with mobility impairments, and children — at the moment they cross. The platform is being designed to support partnerships with city and county councils across Ireland.
