Story · Cork, Ireland

The crossing that was fixed in five days.

A junction in Ballincollig was measured by one person with a smartphone. Five days later, Cork City Council confirmed two pedestrian push buttons had been repaired — and admitted the signal timing hadn't been revised in 13 years. This is what changed.

22 April 2026 · By Emmett Murphy · 6 min read

The junction of Killumney Road and Carriganarra Road in Ballincollig is unremarkable. It sits in a quiet part of west Cork county, surrounded by housing and a school. Most people who cross it have done so thousands of times, without thinking about it.

That's part of the problem.

Pedestrian crossings are designed on the assumption that everyone walks at 1.0 metres per second. It's an engineering convention drawn from research that's now decades old. It applies to junctions across Ireland, the UK, Europe, and most of the developed world. It assumes a fit adult, walking briskly, on a clear day.

Most people don't walk that fast. Older adults walk slower. People with mobility impairments walk slower. Parents pushing prams walk slower. People carrying shopping walk slower. Children walk slower. The number isn't fringe — research from agencies including the U.S. Federal Highway Administration consistently puts the real walking speed of vulnerable pedestrians somewhere between 0.6 and 0.8 metres per second.

That gap matters. At a typical urban crossing, a fit adult walking at 1.0 m/s might cross 12 metres of road in 12 seconds. Someone walking at 0.7 m/s needs 17 seconds. If the green walk phase was set with the first number in mind, the second person is still in the road when the signal changes.

The walk

On Friday, 17 April 2026, a WalkPhase user crossed the Killumney Road junction. The smartphone in their pocket recorded what happened: when they arrived at the kerb, when they began crossing, how fast they walked, and how long the signal gave them.

Within seconds of arriving at the other side, the app had a structured safety report ready. Walking speed. Wait time. Signal phase duration. Comparison to the engineering assumption. A clear summary of what was wrong.

The user sent the report to Cork City Council's Traffic Requests team that evening.

Five days later, on Wednesday, 22 April 2026, the ITS Section of Cork City Council responded.

Cork City Council — written response · Ref: CSR/2026/08609/RDS

"Hi Emmett, I got confirmation that two push buttons were repaired at that junction… Another visit is planned to confirm crossing distances and associated times as the parameters for that junction likely haven't been revised since its original installation 13 odd years ago."

— ITS Section, Cork City Council, 22 April 2026

Two specific issues had been identified, escalated, and fixed in less time than it takes most people to receive a reply from a council planning office. But the more striking part of the response was the second sentence.

The signal timing parameters at this junction hadn't been revised in 13 years. The pedestrians who walked the slowest in 2013 are not the pedestrians using the crossing in 2026. The crossing wasn't unsafe because anyone made a mistake. It was unsafe because the assumptions baked into it had been left to age, while the people relying on them changed.

The Council did not act on a complaint. They acted on evidence. There was no debate about whether the crossing was safe. The data made the answer self-evident.

"If a crossing isn't safe, you shouldn't have to argue. You should be able to prove it."

Emmett Murphy, founder, WalkPhase

Ireland's Government Road Safety Strategy 2021–2030 commits to halving road deaths and serious injuries by 2030, and to a Vision Zero outcome of zero deaths or serious injuries on Irish roads by 2050. Pedestrian fatalities are moving in the wrong direction.

It's not because nobody is doing anything. The Road Safety Authority publishes detailed quarterly reports. Local councils run safety campaigns. National media run stories every year. Advocates write letters. People file complaints.

But councils don't fix crossings because someone wrote a letter. They fix them because someone produced evidence — and the evidence makes the answer obvious.

"Cities don't ignore data. They ignore complaints."

Emmett Murphy, founder, WalkPhase

What WalkPhase does

WalkPhase is a smartphone-based platform that measures real walking speeds, wait times, and signal timing at signalised crossings. It uses AI to turn that data into a structured safety report — the kind of evidence councils need but rarely receive.

The crucial design choice: no roadside hardware. No sensors on poles. No installation. No infrastructure required. Any signalised crossing in Ireland can be measured by anyone with a phone.

That choice matters. Traditional pedestrian safety studies require a council, a budget, a procurement process, a contractor, and weeks or months of fieldwork — for a single junction. WalkPhase reduces that to a single walk by a single person, anywhere in the country.

The phone goes in the pedestrian's pocket. Sensors are passive. The user doesn't need to look at a screen while crossing. WalkPhase isn't a tool people use during a crossing — it's a measurement system that observes what's already happening.

What happens next

The Killumney Road junction is the first publicly verifiable example of a WalkPhase-driven outcome. It will not be the last.

The platform is being designed to support partnerships with city and county councils across Ireland — providing a continuous stream of real-world pedestrian safety evidence drawn from the people who use their crossings, not occasional consultancy studies.

Future development includes signal-controller integration to automatically extend green-walk time for verified vulnerable road users at the moment they cross — older adults, people with mobility impairments, children. The signals respond to the people, not to a thirty-year-old engineering assumption.

Ireland is where this was first proved to work. If it works at one crossing, it works at every crossing. And if it works in Ireland, it works anywhere.

"This isn't one faulty crossing. It's how crossings are designed."

Emmett Murphy, founder, WalkPhase
